What is Melatonin?

Melatonin is an indoleamine hormone produced by the pineal gland in response to darkness. It binds to MT1 and MT2 receptors in the suprachiasmatic nucleus to entrain the sleep-wake cycle. Peak levels occur between 2–4 AM, facilitating sleep onset and maintenance. Its lipophilic nature enables blood-brain barrier crossing, affecting multiple brain regions.

Exogenous melatonin comes in various forms—immediate-release, timed-release, sublingual drops, and transdermal patches—to finely tune absorption and duration for specific sleep challenges.

History & Traditional Use

Though melatonin itself was isolated in 1958, natural analogs have been used for millennia. Ancient Ayurvedic texts reference extracts from plants like amla and St. John’s wort to induce sleep. In modern times, melatonin supplements gained popularity in the 1990s for jet lag. Chronobiologists have since embraced melatonin for shift-work adaptation and seasonal adjustment therapies.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, nocturnal herbs such as Ziziphus jujuba (suan zao ren) complement melatonin’s effects, promoting Yin to counter arboreal imbalances in sleeping patterns.
