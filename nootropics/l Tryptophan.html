What is L-Tryptophan?

L-Tryptophan is one of the nine essential amino acids that humans must obtain from diet or supplementation. It serves as the starting point for synthesizing serotonin (the “feel-good” neurotransmitter), melatonin (the sleep hormone), and niacin (vitamin B3), which is crucial for energy metabolism and nervous system integrity.

History & Traditional Use

First isolated in the early 20th century, L-Tryptophan quickly became recognized for its role in protein synthesis and nitrogen balance. In traditional cultures, tryptophan-rich foods like turkey and cheese were prized for their soothing effects on mood and sleep, long before the pathways were fully mapped by modern biochemistry.

Fun Facts & Quick Tips

  • Turkey owes its reputation as a sleep-inducer partially to its tryptophan content—but portion sizes matter!
  • Pairing L-Tryptophan with carbohydrates boosts its transport across the blood-brain barrier.
  • Niacin synthesis from tryptophan requires B6, so co-supplement with pyridoxal-5-phosphate.
  • Avoid high-protein meals right after dosing to reduce competition for amino acid transporters.

Side Effects & Safety Considerations
